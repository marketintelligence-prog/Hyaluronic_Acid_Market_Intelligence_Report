Product Overview & Identity
Chemical identity, the acid-vs-salt distinction, properties and grades

What it is — and why "acid" and "salt" are traded as one

Hyaluronic acid (HA) is a naturally occurring, high-molecular-weight linear polysaccharide (a glycosaminoglycan) built from repeating disaccharide units of D-glucuronic acid and N-acetyl-D-glucosamine. In its free-acid form it is chemically unstable and impractical to isolate or store, so virtually all commercial "hyaluronic acid" is in fact its sodium salt — sodium hyaluronate. This is the single most important commercial fact for a trader: the two are sold, invoiced and shipped almost interchangeably, which is exactly why the supplied workbook groups them as one product line.

Key focus point — the acid vs. salt difference (secondary-research basis) The free acid and the sodium salt are the same polymer backbone; the salt simply has the carboxyl protons replaced by sodium. In practice the differences that matter commercially are: (1) Stability & handling — sodium hyaluronate is a stable, hygroscopic white powder with ~3-year shelf life, whereas free HA degrades faster; (2) Solubility — the salt dissolves readily in cold water to a clear viscous gel, making it the preferred form for cosmetics, eye-drops and injectables; (3) Regulatory naming — pharmacopoeias and the cosmetic INCI system list "Sodium Hyaluronate," so finished-product labels and DMFs almost always cite the salt; (4) Molecular weight is the real price driver, not acid-vs-salt — grades run from ultra-low-MW oligomers to >2 million Da. For sourcing, import and trading purposes they can be treated as one commodity with two CAS numbers.

Synthesis & Manufacturing
Routes, raw materials and where India can play

How it is made

Over 95% of commercial HA is produced by microbial fermentation, which has largely displaced the older rooster-comb animal-extraction route on grounds of purity, batch consistency, vegan labelling and traceability.

#RouteStarting materials & conditionsNotes / Reference
1Microbial fermentation (bio-based — industry standard)Streptococcus zooepidemicus or engineered Bacillus subtilis; submerged aerobic fermentation in 50,000–100,000 L stirred-tank bioreactors (glucose/peptone feed), controlled T/pH/DO[22]

India regulatory summary (plain language) For an import/trading/sourcing business, bulk HA and sodium hyaluronate are effectively unrestricted — a normal IEC, standard customs duty + GST, and you can trade freely. The regulatory bar rises only if you handle sterile injectables, dermal fillers or ophthalmic viscoelastics, which are medical devices/drugs requiring CDSCO registration. Cosmetic serums fall under the Cosmetics Rules; oral supplements under FSSAI. This makes the low-regulation trading/sourcing modes the natural entry point.
